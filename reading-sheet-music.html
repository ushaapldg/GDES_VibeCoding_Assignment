Reading Notes

Every note on a staff has a letter name (A through G) determined by where it sits. The clef at the start of the staff sets which line is which — so the same position means a different note in treble vs. bass clef.

Treble Clef Notes

The treble clef curls around the G line (line 2 from the bottom), fixing that line as G4. All other note positions follow from there going up and down by step.

Lines — bottom to top
E4 G4 B4 D5 F5
Every Good Boy Does Fine
Spaces — bottom to top
F4 A4 C5 E5
F — A — C — E
Middle C C4 (middle C) sits on a short ledger line just below the treble staff. It's the most important landmark note — once you know it, you can count up or down to find any other note.
Bass Clef Notes

The bass clef's two dots sit above and below the F line (line 4 from the bottom), fixing that line as F3. Bass clef covers the lower range used by instruments like cello, bass guitar, and the piano's left hand.

Lines — bottom to top
G2 B2 D3 F3 A3
Good Boys Do Fine Always
Spaces — bottom to top
A2 C3 E3 G3
All Cows Eat Grass
Middle C C4 (middle C) sits on a ledger line just above the bass staff — the mirror image of its position below the treble staff. It's the note where the two clefs meet.
Accidentals

An accidental is a symbol placed directly before a note head to alter its pitch. There are five accidentals, but the three below cover almost everything you'll encounter as a beginner.

♯
Sharp Raises the note by one half step (one semitone).
♭
Flat Lowers the note by one half step (one semitone).
♮
Natural Cancels any sharp or flat — plays the plain note.
Scope An accidental applies to every note on that line or space for the rest of the measure. The next bar line cancels it automatically. If you want it to continue into the next measure, the composer must write it again.
Enharmonics Two notes can sound identical but be written differently — F♯ and G♭ are the same pitch. These are called enharmonic equivalents. Which spelling is used depends on the key signature and context.
Key Signatures

A key signature appears right after the clef at the start of every line of music. It lists the sharps or flats that apply throughout the entire piece, so you don't have to mark every individual note. Reading the key signature first tells you which notes are always altered.

Order of sharps F C G D A E B — Father Charles Goes Down And Ends Battle. Sharps are always added in this order; a 3-sharp key signature always contains F♯, C♯, and G♯.
Order of flats B E A D G C F — Battle Ends And Down Goes Charles's Father (the reverse of the sharps). A 3-flat key signature always contains B♭, E♭, and A♭.

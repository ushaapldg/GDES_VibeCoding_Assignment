What Each Part Means
1
Title The name of the piece, centred at the top of the first page. Usually the largest text on the page.
2
Composer & Arranger The person who wrote the music (top right). An arranger's name appears below if the original was adapted for a different instrument or ensemble.
3
Tempo Marking An Italian word (e.g. Andante, Allegro) and/or a metronome marking (♩= 88) above the first staff, indicating how fast the piece should be played.
4
Instrument Name Printed to the left of the first staff to identify which instrument or voice that line of music is for. Only shown on the first system of each page.
5
Clef The symbol at the start of every staff that sets which pitches the lines and spaces represent. The treble clef (𝄞) is used for higher ranges; the bass clef (𝄢) for lower ones.
6
Key Signature Sharps or flats placed right after the clef. They apply to the matching notes throughout the entire piece, so you don't need to mark every individual note. No sharps or flats means C major.
7
Time Signature Two stacked numbers after the key signature. The top number tells you how many beats are in each measure; the bottom tells you which note value gets one beat (4 = quarter note).
8
Bar Lines & Measures Vertical lines that divide the staff into measures (bars). Each measure contains exactly the number of beats shown in the time signature. A double bar line marks a section; a final bar line ends the piece.
9
Dynamic Marking An Italian abbreviation below the staff indicating volume — from pp (very soft) through ff (very loud). Changes in dynamics can appear anywhere in the music.
10
Measure Numbers Small numbers above the first measure of each system that tell you which measure you're at. Useful for rehearsals — a conductor can say "from bar 5" and everyone finds the same place instantly.
11
Copyright & Page Number The copyright notice (bottom left or centre) states who owns the music and the year of publication. The page number (bottom right) helps when navigating longer pieces.

Music Dictionary

Volume markings — how loud or soft to play
𝑝𝑝
Pianissimo
Italian: "very soft"
Play very quietly. Two degrees below forte.
𝑝
Piano
Italian: "soft"
Play softly. One of the most common dynamic markings.
𝑚𝑝
Mezzo-piano
Italian: "half soft"
Moderately soft — louder than p, softer than mf.
𝑚𝑓
Mezzo-forte
Italian: "half loud"
Moderately loud. Often the natural, conversational volume.
𝑓
Forte
Italian: "strong / loud"
Play loudly. One of the most common dynamic markings.
𝑓𝑓
Fortissimo
Italian: "very loud"
Play very loudly. Two degrees above piano.
𝑠𝑓𝑧
Sforzando
Italian: "forcing"
A sudden, sharp accent on a single note or chord.
𝑓𝑝
Forte-piano
Italian: "loud then soft"
Attack strongly, then immediately drop to soft.
Crescendo
Italian: "growing"
Gradually get louder. Shown as an opening hairpin: <
Decrescendo
Italian: "decreasing"
Gradually get softer. Also called diminuendo. Shown as a closing hairpin: >
Speed markings — how fast or slow to play
Grave
Grave
Italian: "grave / solemn"
Very slow and solemn. The slowest standard tempo (~40 bpm).
Largo
Largo
Italian: "broad"
Very slow and broad (~45–50 bpm).
Adagio
Adagio
Italian: "at ease"
Slow and stately (~66–76 bpm). Often used in expressive movements.
Andante
Andante
Italian: "walking"
A moderate walking pace (~76–108 bpm).
Moderato
Moderato
Italian: "moderate"
A moderate tempo (~108–120 bpm).
Allegretto
Allegretto
Italian: "a little lively"
Moderately fast, lighter than Allegro (~112–120 bpm).
Allegro
Allegro
Italian: "cheerful / fast"
Fast and lively (~120–156 bpm). Very common tempo marking.
Vivace
Vivace
Italian: "lively / vivacious"
Quick and spirited (~156–176 bpm).
Presto
Presto
Italian: "quickly"
Very fast (~168–200 bpm). One of the fastest standard tempos.
accel.
Accelerando
Italian: "speeding up"
Gradually increase the tempo. Abbreviated accel.
rit.
Ritardando
Italian: "slowing down"
Gradually slow down. Abbreviated rit. or ritard.
a tempo
A tempo
Italian: "in time"
Return to the original tempo after a deviation like rit. or accel.
rubato
Rubato
Italian: "robbed time"
Perform with flexible, expressive timing — speeding up and slowing down freely.
How individual notes are attacked, held, or shaped
Staccato
Italian: "detached"
Play the note short and detached. Shown as a dot above or below the notehead.
Legato / Slur
Italian: "tied / smooth"
Play notes smoothly and connected. Shown as a curved slur line over a group of notes.
Accent
Latin: "to add a tone"
Emphasize this note with extra force. Shown as a > symbol above the note.
Tenuto
Italian: "held"
Hold the note for its full value, with a gentle emphasis. Shown as a short horizontal line.
Marcato
Italian: "marked"
Play the note with a strong, emphasized attack. Shown as a caret (^) above the note.
Fermata
Italian: "stop / pause"
Hold the note longer than its written value, at the performer's discretion.
Trill
Italian: "trillo"
Rapidly alternate between the written note and the note a step above it. Marked tr with a wavy line.
Staccatissimo
Italian: "very detached"
Even shorter than staccato. Shown as a wedge or narrow dot above the note.
Character and mood instructions
dolce
Dolce
Italian: "sweet"
Play in a sweet, gentle, and tender manner.
espr.
Espressivo
Italian: "expressive"
With expression and feeling. Abbreviated espr.
cantabile
Cantabile
Italian: "singable"
In a singing style — lyrical, flowing, and melodic.
grazioso
Grazioso
Italian: "graceful"
Play in a graceful, elegant manner.
con brio
Con brio
Italian: "with vigor"
With energy, spirit, and enthusiasm.
leggiero
Leggiero
Italian: "light"
Play lightly and delicately, with a nimble touch.
maestoso
Maestoso
Italian: "majestic"
In a majestic, stately, and dignified manner.
agitato
Agitato
Italian: "agitated"
Play in a restless, agitated, or excited manner.
tranquillo
Tranquillo
Italian: "tranquil"
Calm and peaceful. Play quietly and without tension.
con fuoco
Con fuoco
Italian: "with fire"
With fire and passion — intense and brilliant.
mesto
Mesto
Italian: "sad"
In a mournful or melancholy character.
giocoso
Giocoso
Italian: "playful"
In a humorous, playful, or jocular manner.
Repeat and navigation symbols
Repeat Signs
Music notation
Go back to the opening repeat barline (or the beginning) and play the section again.
𝄋
Segno
Italian: "sign"
A landmark symbol. Used with D.S. to jump back to this sign.
𝄌
Coda
Italian: "tail"
A closing section of a piece. Jump to this symbol when instructed by al Coda.
D.C.
Da Capo
Italian: "from the head"
Return to the very beginning of the piece and play again. Abbreviated D.C.
D.S.
Dal Segno
Italian: "from the sign"
Return to the Segno (𝄋) symbol and play from there. Abbreviated D.S.
Fine
Fine
Italian: "end"
The end of the piece. Often used with D.C. al Fine or D.S. al Fine.
Volta Brackets
Italian: "time"
1st and 2nd endings. On the first pass play the 1st bracket; on the repeat skip it and play the 2nd.
al Coda
Al Coda
Italian: "to the tail"
Jump forward to the Coda symbol (𝄌). Used with D.C. or D.S.: e.g. D.C. al Coda.
Caesura
Latin: "cutting"
A brief, complete pause or break in the music. Also called a "railroad tracks" or "grand pause" mark.
